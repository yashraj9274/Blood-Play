I remember being four years old, walking through the dark alleys of the market and stopping outside a little wooden shed. My birthday was the next day, and I was convinced Father was finally going to get me the diary I'd been begging for, the one I wanted to fill with sketches and notes about all the animals I met. (I still carry that diary. The drawings have gotten much better. The reasons for drawing them became worse.)
(Birthdays were a luxury we couldn't really afford. Except for Suzanne. She was the eldest among us, the responsible one, the golden one, the one who'd disappear for days and somehow come back with enough money to keep us all alive for a little while. Never enough to feed all twelve for long, though.)
I remember sitting there for what felt like forever, watching Father talk to a large man with an orange beard. Then Father stood up, gave me one last look, and walked away. The large man picked me up, sat me on his shoulders, bought me the diary I'd wanted for so long... and even let me have meat. In fact, that was all I ate for the next twenty years.
I became part of a hunter's pack. I hunted animals, sold them, skinned them, ate them, and wrote about every one of them in my diary, my own little bestiary, if you will, until one day.

I was tracking an owlbear I'd wounded when it disappeared into a thicket and let out a low groan. I crept closer and peeked through the bushes, and there she was, sitting beside it, gently stroking its head until it calmed down enough for her to pull my arrow from its shoulder. Then she snapped the arrow in half and threw it into the fire. I still don't know what I felt. I'd seen beautiful people before. Brave people too. Kind people as well. I'd just never seen all of them in the same person. Before I even realized she'd moved, she was standing beside me with one hand on my shoulder and somehow held me in place. Then she spent what felt like the next hour lecturing me about what I was doing to the forest, the animals, and apparently to myself. Strangely enough, I stayed.

After that day, I helped her with whatever needed doing. We gathered herbs, patched fences, looked after injured animals, helped people around the village, and mostly just wandered around while she talked about nature and I listened. Somewhere along the way, listening became my favorite thing to do. I still left every now and then to collect bounties and restock supplies, but every journey ended with me coming back to her.
One evening I showed her my bestiary. She laughed in that quiet little way she always did and started helping me fill the empty pages.
About a year later the tribe accepted me as one of their own. She was the one who initiated me. I sat there for hours while she covered my back in some magical nature thingy. I still don't know what it was. I just know that when she finished, the markings somehow gave me my abilities.

That wasn't the only thing she gave me. Later that year she gave me another little gift, Sherlyn.

The three of us spent our days helping the village, tending to wounded animals, and making life a little easier for anyone who needed it. I still took the occasional bounty whenever supplies ran low, but apart from that, life was peaceful.
About two years later, when Sherlyn was three, the tribe had begun preparing for her initiation, the ceremony that would welcome her as one of their own. I left to gather supplies for it, expecting to be gone only a few days.
When I got back, Simba found me at the edge of the forest carrying Sherlyn carefully by the cloth wrapped around her back. With soft, desperate whimpers, he turned and led me toward the village. I thought I'd return home to a celebration. Instead, I found death, decay, and madness.

The first thing I noticed was how quiet everything was. Villages aren't supposed to be quiet. There were no birds, no voices, no laughter. Cooking fires had burned themselves out, doors hung open, children's toys were scattered in the mud, and then I started recognizing the people lying beside them. I stepped past faces I recognized. The elder had died fighting. The survivors were in no condition to tell me what had happened, but Simba and his pack did. Selena was still alive... or at least part of her was. The moment she saw me, she lunged. There wasn't even a flicker of recognition in her eyes, only hunger. I restrained her and built a cage strong enough to hold her. It was never meant to be a cure, just enough time for me to find one.

We buried who we could, burned those we couldn't, and rebuilt what little remained. Somewhere along the way people stopped calling the village by its old name. They started calling it Carcosa. I never argued with them.
People say Carcosa is where hope goes to die, they're wrong, hope's still there. It's just a little harder to feed than Selena is.
It's been ten years now. Sherlyn has grown into someone who reminds me more and more of her mother every day. She's stubborn, kind, clever, and far braver than she has any right to be. She doesn't remember the woman Selena used to be, only the one behind the bars.
I stopped hunting animals that day, humans pay better anyway.
Every bounty I've taken since then has only ever had one purpose.
Finding a cure.
