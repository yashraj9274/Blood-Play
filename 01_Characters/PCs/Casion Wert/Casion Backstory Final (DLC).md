### Act 1 ( Childhood and Youth)

I remember being four years old, walking through the rain-soaked alleys of the market and stopping outside a little wooden stall. My birthday was the next day, and I was convinced Father was finally going to buy me the diary I'd been begging for. I wanted somewhere to draw all the animals I kept seeing. Birds, foxes, stray dogs... anything that wandered into my path. _(I still carry that diary. The drawings have gotten much better. The reasons for drawing them became worse.)_ Birthdays weren't really something my family could afford. There were twelve of us. Suzanne was the eldest—the responsible one. Somehow she'd disappear for days and always come back with just enough coin to keep us alive a little longer. I never questioned where it came from. That day Father spent a long time talking to a huge man with an orange beard. Eventually he stood up, looked at me one last time... and walked away. The man bought me the diary I'd wanted so badly. He even bought me meat. I thought Father would come back when he was finished. He never did. Torvald—that was the man's name—hadn't kidnapped me. He'd collected a debt my father couldn't pay. So I paid it instead.

For the next twenty years, I belonged to a hunter's pack. We tracked anything that would feed people or fetch enough coin to keep the camp alive. Deer, wolves, bears, boars... sometimes things far worse. The first animal I ever skinned was a deer. I cried so hard I couldn't see what I was doing. After a while... it got easier. Or maybe I just got better at pretending it did. Every hunt ended the same way. I'd clean my knife, sharpen my arrows, then spend half the night filling that little diary with sketches and notes about whatever I'd killed that day. I told myself I was becoming a better hunter by noticing those things. The others thought it was strange. A hunter carrying a diary was good for a laugh. So I stopped showing it to anyone. Everyone except Torvald. He never laughed. He didn't understand why I drew, but he never told me to stop either.

My father's debt was paid when I was fifteen. Torvald sat me down, handed me a drink, and told me I was free to leave whenever I wanted. I asked him where I was supposed to go. He didn't have an answer, and neither did I. Somewhere along the way that camp had become my home. Not because I loved hunting. Truth be told, I don't think I ever did. I stayed because I finally belonged somewhere, and because every compliment, every nod of approval, every "well done" felt like proof that I'd earned my place there. So I hunted. I became good at it. Better than most. I told myself I was happy. Then, twenty years after I'd first picked up a bow, I wounded an owlbear, followed its trail into the forest... and met the woman who spent the rest of her life proving I had been lying to myself.



### Act 2 ( Happiest Few Years)

The first time I saw Selena, she was pulling my arrow out of an owlbear I'd been tracking for half the day. She calmed it down, snapped my arrow in half, threw it into the fire, then spent the next hour explaining why I was an idiot. I should've been annoyed. Instead, I went back the next day. Then the day after that. Somewhere along the way, talking became listening, and listening became my favorite thing to do. She knew more about animals than anyone I'd ever met, and I'd stop her every few minutes to scribble something into my diary before I forgot it. She never laughed. She'd read through the pages, add little notes in the margins, and somehow make me feel like they were worth writing. She was also the first person to realize I didn't just like drawing animals—I loved them. When she asked why I didn't leave the hunters if that was true, I told her it was home. The next day she walked straight into the camp, sat down with Torvald, and learned more about me than I'd ever managed to tell anyone myself. She never tried to change me after that. She just stayed.

The months that followed were probably the happiest of my life. We'd spend our days gathering herbs, helping people around the village, tending to injured animals, and wandering the forest while she talked and I listened. Every now and then she'd nearly fall off a cliff or get herself bitten by something that shouldn't have had teeth, and I'd get to save her just often enough that she couldn't tease me for worrying too much. I'd carve little animals out of wood for her whenever I had spare time, and she'd treasure them like they belonged in a palace instead of a pocket. She listened when I spoke, read every page of my diary without judging it, and somehow made me feel like I didn't have to earn being cared about anymore. I don't remember exactly when I fell in love with her. I just remember realizing one day that every road I walked, I hoped it ended where she was just looking forward to seeing her tomorrow... then I realized I'd been doing that for months.

About a year later the tribe accepted me as one of their own. She was the one who initiated me. I sat there for hours while she covered my back in some magical nature thingy. I still don't know what it was. I just know that when she finished, the markings somehow gave me my abilities. That wasn't the only thing she gave me that year. Later she smiled at me in that quiet little way she always did and told me we were going to have a daughter. We named her Sherlyn. The three of us spent the next few years helping the village, tending to wounded animals, and making life a little easier for anyone who needed it. I still left every now and then to collect a bounty or restock supplies. But for the first time in my life... every road led me somewhere I couldn't wait to return to.


### Act 3 (Death Decay and Derangement)

I left the village only a few days before Sherlyn's third birthday. The tribe had spent weeks preparing for her initiation, and I wanted everything to be perfect. I gathered supplies, picked up a few little gifts I'd been hiding from both of them, and spent the whole journey thinking about the look on Sherlyn's face when she opened them. I should've come home a day earlier. Instead, Simba found me at the edge of the forest with Sherlyn wrapped carefully in a cloth hanging from his mouth. He let out a soft, desperate whimper before turning around, and his whole pack followed. I ran after them. The first thing I noticed was how quiet everything was. Villages aren't supposed to be quiet. There were no birds. No voices. No laughter. Cooking fires had burned themselves out, doors hung open, children's toys lay scattered in the mud, and people I had shared meals with lay where they had fallen. I didn't stop for any of them. My legs carried me straight home. Selena was still there... or at least part of her was. For one impossible moment I thought I'd made it in time. Then she looked at me. There wasn't even a flicker of recognition in her eyes. She screamed and lunged. I restrained her, built a cage strong enough to hold her, buried the people I could, burned the ones I couldn't, and spent the following months rebuilding everything else. The others who had turned became part of the village's defenses, wandering the outskirts where few dared to travel. Somewhere along the way, people stopped calling the village by its old name. They started calling it **Carcosa**. I never argued with them.

Most people think I'm cruel for keeping Selena alive. Maybe they would be right if all they ever saw was the cage. They don't see her wake before sunrise at the same hour she always used to, or the way she quietly watches flowers swaying in the wind. They don't see her rest a hand on Simba's head when he lies beside her, or hear her hum the same tune she used to sing while tending herbs before it fades away again. They don't see her watch children playing outside the walls for hours, or the way she tilts her head whenever Sherlyn laughs. None of those things prove she's still in there. But they stop me from believing she's gone. So every time I return from a bounty, I sit beside her for a while and tell her about the road, about the people I met, and about Sherlyn. Maybe she's listening. Maybe she isn't. I can't bring myself to stop trying.

Sherlyn doesn't remember the woman Selena used to be. She only knows the one behind the bars. Even so, she's becoming more like her mother every year. She has the same kindness, the same stubbornness, and the same habit of asking questions that don't have easy answers. I was never an absent father. Every bounty I took lasted only a few days before I came home again. We repaired houses together, tended the forest together, helped anyone brave enough to visit Carcosa, and searched for answers together. I stopped hunting animals that day. Humans pay better anyway, and they leave behind journals, rumors, and secrets that beasts never do. Every bounty became another chance to learn something about the sickness, another clue, another failed cure written into the same diary that once held nothing but sketches of wildlife.Every bounty I've taken since has only ever had one purpose. Finding a cure.

People say Carcosa is where hope goes to die.
They're wrong.
Hope's still there.
It's just a little harder to feed than Selena is.
